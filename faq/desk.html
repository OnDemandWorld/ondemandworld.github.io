1. The Basics — What Is Desk?

9 questions

Desk is an AI-powered customer support agent that lives on WhatsApp (and other messaging channels). Think of it as a very knowledgeable front-desk receptionist who never sleeps — it reads your company's documents, understands your products and policies, and answers customer questions automatically, 24/7.

When a question is too complex or sensitive for the AI, it hands the conversation over to a human agent — with full context so the human doesn't have to start from scratch.

Crucially, Desk runs on your own infrastructure. Your customer conversations never touch third-party servers unless you explicitly choose to use a cloud AI model.

Three problems, in order of importance:

  1. Data sovereignty. Customer support conversations contain names, phone numbers, order details, health information, financial data. Cloud-based tools (Intercom, Zendesk AI, Ada) send all of this to third-party servers — often in jurisdictions that conflict with data-residency laws like GDPR, HIPAA, or PDPA. Desk keeps everything on your own servers.
  2. WhatsApp is underserved. WhatsApp is the dominant support channel in most of the world outside the US (2+ billion users). Yet most self-hosted support tools bolt WhatsApp on as an afterthought. Desk is designed WhatsApp-first.
  3. Repetitive questions overwhelm teams. Most support teams spend 60-80% of their time answering the same questions. Desk automates this, freeing humans for complex, high-value conversations.

ChatGPT knows a lot about the general internet but knows nothing about your company. Ask it "What's your return policy?" and it will guess or refuse.

Desk is the opposite: it knows your documents inside out. It retrieves answers from your own knowledge base (product manuals, policy documents, FAQs) and can cite the exact source. It also speaks in your brand voice, follows your response policies, and keeps data on your servers.

Think of ChatGPT as a brilliant stranger, and Desk as a trained employee who has read your entire employee handbook.

"Self-hosted" means Desk runs on your own servers — either a machine in your office, a private cloud you control (AWS, Azure, GCP), or a server rented from a hosting provider. No one else has access to the hardware.

This matters because:

  • Privacy: Customer conversation data never leaves your infrastructure (unless you choose to route some queries to cloud AI models).
  • Compliance: You can satisfy data-residency requirements (GDPR, HIPAA, PDPA) because you control where data physically lives.
  • No vendor lock-in: You own the deployment. If you stop paying for the software license, your data is still there, exportable, yours.
Analogy Cloud SaaS is like renting a hotel room — convenient, but the hotel owns everything. Self-hosted is like owning your house — more responsibility, but total control.

A conversation is a continuous thread of messages between a customer and Desk (or a human agent). It starts when a customer sends their first message and ends when the conversation is marked as resolved.

Each conversation has a state: it can be active (AI is handling it), escalated (a human agent has taken over), pending (waiting for customer reply), or resolved (closed). The state machine ensures nothing falls through the cracks.

"Model-agnostic" means Desk doesn't force you to use one specific AI brain. You can choose:

  • Local models (e.g., Llama, Mistral) — run entirely on your own hardware. Maximum privacy, no per-message cost.
  • Frontier models (e.g., GPT-4, Claude) — cloud-based, higher quality for complex questions, but data leaves your server.
  • Hybrid routing — Desk automatically picks the right model per query. PII-heavy questions stay local; complex reasoning goes to frontier models.

You're not locked into any single AI provider. Switch models anytime without changing your setup.

ODW.ai is a suite of AI products for businesses. Desk is one module in that suite. The most important sibling is Vault — the suite's knowledge base and document intelligence platform.

Desk integrates deeply with Vault: when a customer asks a question, Desk queries Vault to find the relevant documents, then generates an answer grounded in those documents. If you already use Vault, Desk works in minutes — your knowledge base is already there.

Desk also shares authentication (single sign-on), user management, and billing with the rest of the suite.

Three primary audiences:

  1. Regulated businesses (10–500 employees) — healthcare clinics, legal firms, fintech startups, government contractors, education institutions. These organizations need data sovereignty and can't send customer conversations to cloud SaaS.
  2. Businesses in regions with strict data-localization laws — EU (GDPR), Brazil (LGPD), India, Southeast Asia (PDPA). Desk lets you keep data within your jurisdiction.
  3. Developer-led companies and agencies — teams that want to white-label or deeply customize their support stack without vendor lock-in.

Desk is not designed for businesses that need 200+ integrations, visual flow builders, or full CRM functionality. It's focused on AI-first support with sovereignty.

Most open-source support tools (Chatwoot, Botpress) add WhatsApp as an afterthought — a connector bolted onto a web-chat-first architecture. This leads to awkward UX: message templates don't work properly, the 24-hour window isn't tracked, agent handoff is clunky.

Desk was designed from day one around WhatsApp's constraints and strengths:

  • 24-hour session window — tracked natively. Desk knows when the window expires and switches to template messages automatically.
  • Message templates — built-in management for approved templates (order confirmations, appointment reminders).
  • Rich media — images, documents, location sharing all handled as first-class message types.
  • Agent handoff — designed for the WhatsApp experience, not adapted from a web chat paradigm.

The WhatsApp Business API is Meta's official channel for businesses to message customers at scale. It's the same API that large companies use for order notifications, appointment reminders, and customer support.

To get access:

  1. Create a Meta Business Manager account (if you don't have one)
  2. Verify your business with Meta (requires business documents — registration certificate, utility bill)
  3. Request WhatsApp Business API access through Meta or a Business Solution Provider (BSP)
  4. Wait for approval (typically 1-5 business days)

Once approved, Desk connects directly to the API. You get official green-tick verification (optional), message templates, and full Meta support.

Heads up Meta charges per conversation (not per message). Pricing varies by country and conversation type (marketing, utility, service). Budget ~$0.03-$0.08 per conversation in most markets.

WhatsApp's Business API has a rule: once a customer sends you a message, you have 24 hours to reply with free-form messages. After 24 hours, the "session window" closes and you can only send pre-approved template messages (e.g., "Your order has shipped") until the customer messages you again.

Desk tracks this window automatically. If a conversation is approaching the 24-hour limit, Desk will:

  • Switch to template messages for proactive outreach
  • Alert your agent inbox that the session is expiring
  • Log the window state for compliance auditing

You don't need to manage this manually — Desk handles it.

When a customer sends a message, here's what happens behind the scenes:

  1. Channel Gateway — receives the message from WhatsApp (or other channel) and normalizes it into a standard format.
  2. Message Router — identifies the customer, loads their conversation history, and decides: should AI handle this, or a human?
  3. Conversation Manager — saves the message, updates the conversation state, and starts SLA timers (how long has the customer been waiting?).
  4. PII Shield — scans the message for personally identifiable information (names, phone numbers, credit cards). If PII is detected, it's redacted before sending to cloud AI models, or the query is routed to a local model only.
  5. Policy Engine — checks if the message triggers any response policies (e.g., competitor mentions, legal disclaimers). If a hard rule matches, a template response is sent immediately.
  6. Model Router — decides which AI model to use (local or frontier) based on PII detection, complexity, and your routing policy.
  7. Persona Service — composes the system prompt with your brand voice (tone, vocabulary, do's/don'ts).
  8. Vault Client — queries your knowledge base (Vault) for relevant documents. Retrieves the top 5-10 most relevant chunks.
  9. AI Engine — sends the customer's question + retrieved documents + brand persona to the AI model. Gets a response back.
  10. Confidence Scorer — calculates how confident the AI is in its answer. If confidence is below your threshold, the conversation is escalated to a human.
  11. Policy Engine (post-check) — validates the AI's response against your policies. If it violates a rule (e.g., mentions a competitor), it's rewritten or blocked.
  12. Outbound Dispatcher — formats the response for WhatsApp and sends it back to the customer.

Total latency: <5 seconds for local models, <8 seconds for frontier models.

PII stands for Personally Identifiable Information — names, phone numbers, email addresses, credit card numbers, health records, etc.

The PII Shield is a component that scans every message (inbound and outbound) for PII. When it detects PII:

  • Redaction: PII is replaced with placeholders (e.g., "John Smith" → [NAME]) before the message is sent to a cloud AI model.
  • Routing: If PII is detected, the query is automatically routed to a local model only — it never touches a cloud API. This ensures sensitive data stays on your servers.
  • Override: Admins can configure PII patterns (e.g., add industry-specific identifiers) and override routing rules if needed.
Why this matters Cloud AI providers (OpenAI, Anthropic) have strict policies against processing PII. If you accidentally send customer health data to GPT-4, you could violate their terms and HIPAA. The PII Shield prevents this automatically.

After the AI generates a response, Desk calculates a confidence score (0-100%) indicating how sure the AI is that its answer is correct and relevant.

You set a confidence threshold (e.g., 70%). If the AI's confidence is:

  • Above threshold: Response is sent to the customer automatically.
  • Below threshold: Conversation is escalated to a human agent. The AI's draft response is shown to the agent as a suggestion, but the human reviews and sends it.

This ensures the AI only handles questions it's confident about. Complex, ambiguous, or edge-case questions are handed off to humans — no guessing, no hallucination.

Three things can happen, depending on your configuration:

  1. Escalation to human: If confidence is below your threshold, the conversation is escalated. A human agent sees the question + the AI's draft response (if any) and can take over.
  2. Graceful fallback: The AI can be configured to say "I'm not sure about that. Let me connect you with a human who can help" — and then escalate.
  3. Restricted topics mode: You can configure Desk to never answer questions outside your knowledge base. If a question can't be answered from your documents, it's escalated immediately — no guessing.

Desk never makes up answers. If it doesn't know, it says so and hands off.

When a conversation is escalated (low confidence, PII detected, or customer requests a human):

  1. Agent Inbox notification: The conversation appears in the agent inbox with a flag: "Escalated by AI — low confidence" or "PII detected — routed to local model."
  2. Context transfer: The human agent sees the full conversation history, the customer's profile, and the AI's draft response (if any).
  3. Takeover: The agent clicks "Take Over." From that point, the AI stops auto-responding. The human handles the conversation.
  4. Resolution: When the agent resolves the conversation, they mark it as "Resolved." Future messages from that customer go back to the AI (unless you configure it differently).

Agents can also proactively take over any conversation — they don't have to wait for escalation.

The agent inbox is a web-based interface where human agents can:

  • View all conversations (AI-handled, escalated, pending, resolved)
  • Take over conversations from the AI
  • Respond to customers directly
  • See real-time updates via WebSocket (no page refresh needed)
  • Filter conversations by status, channel, customer, or tag
  • View SLA timers (how long has this customer been waiting?)

The inbox is designed for speed — agents can handle multiple conversations in parallel, switch between them instantly, and see AI-suggested responses as drafts.

Desk tracks two key SLA metrics:

  • First-response time: How long does it take for the customer to get a reply after sending their first message? (Target: <5 seconds for AI, <5 minutes for human handoff.)
  • Resolution time: How long does it take to fully resolve the conversation? (Target varies by business.)

SLA timers are enforced automatically. If a conversation exceeds your SLA threshold, Desk can:

  • Alert the agent inbox
  • Escalate to a supervisor
  • Log the SLA breach for reporting

You configure SLA thresholds in the admin dashboard.

4. AI Accuracy & Trust

8 questions

Desk uses multiple layers to prevent hallucination:

  1. Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG): The AI doesn't answer from memory. It retrieves relevant documents from your knowledge base (Vault) and generates answers grounded in those documents. If the documents don't contain the answer, the AI says so.
  2. Confidence scoring: Every response gets a confidence score. Low-confidence responses are escalated to humans — they're never sent to customers automatically.
  3. Policy guardrails: You can configure Desk to never answer questions outside your knowledge base (restricted topics mode). No guessing allowed.
  4. Source citations: Every AI response includes citations to the source documents. Agents (and customers, if you enable it) can verify the answer by reading the original document.
  5. Post-generation validation: The Policy Engine checks every response against your rules. If it violates a policy (e.g., mentions a competitor), it's rewritten or blocked.
Key principle Desk is designed to be honest. If it doesn't know, it says so. It never guesses.

Three safeguards:

  1. Prevention: Confidence scoring + policy guardrails catch most errors before the response is sent.
  2. Detection: If a customer says "That's wrong" or "I disagree," the conversation is flagged for human review.
  3. Correction: Human agents can take over, correct the answer, and mark the conversation as resolved. The correction is logged for future analysis.

You can also review AI-handled conversations in the agent inbox to spot patterns and improve the knowledge base.

This transparency lets you verify the AI's answers and build trust with customers.

Accuracy depends on three factors:

  1. Quality of your knowledge base: If your documents are clear, complete, and up-to-date, the AI will be accurate. Garbage in, garbage out.
  2. Model choice: Frontier models (GPT-4, Claude) are more accurate than local models (Llama, Mistral), but less private.
  3. Configuration: Confidence thresholds, policy guardrails, and restricted topics mode all affect accuracy.

Typical outcomes: 60-80% of conversations are resolved by AI without human intervention. The remaining 20-40% are escalated to humans (either because the AI wasn't confident, or the customer requested a human).

5. Security & Privacy

All data is stored on your own infrastructure:

  • Conversation data: PostgreSQL database (on your server)
  • Media files: S3-compatible storage (your bucket) or local filesystem
  • Knowledge base: Vault (your deployment)
  • Configuration: PostgreSQL database
  • Audit logs: PostgreSQL database

No data is sent to ODW.ai servers. No data is sent to cloud AI providers unless you explicitly configure frontier model routing (and even then, PII is redacted).

Desk is compatible with GDPR, HIPAA, PDPA, and other data-protection regulations — but compliance depends on how you deploy it.

Desk provides the tools you need for compliance:

  • Data sovereignty: All data stays on your servers. You control where it physically lives (important for data-residency requirements).
  • Data export: Export all customer data in standard formats (JSON, CSV) for subject access requests.
  • Data deletion: Delete customer data on request (GDPR "right to erasure"). Deletion is logged in the audit trail.
  • Retention policies: Configure how long data is kept (e.g., "delete conversations after 2 years").
  • Audit logs: Every AI decision, data access, and configuration change is logged.
  • PII Shield: Automatically redacts PII before sending to cloud AI models.
Important Desk gives you the tools, but you are responsible for configuring them correctly and ensuring your deployment meets regulatory requirements. Consult your legal team for specific compliance advice.

Desk uses industry-standard encryption:

  • In transit: TLS 1.3 for all network communication (HTTPS, WebSocket, database connections).
  • At rest: AES-256 encryption for sensitive data in the database (e.g., customer PII, API keys). PostgreSQL Transparent Data Encryption (TDE) or filesystem encryption for the entire database.
  • WhatsApp Baileys sessions: Encrypted auth credentials stored in the database.

You can also enable end-to-end encryption for specific use cases (e.g., healthcare) by configuring additional encryption layers.

The PII Shield scans every message for personally identifiable information:

Desk maintains a tamper-evident audit log of every significant event:

Audit logs are stored in the PostgreSQL database and can be exported to SIEM systems (Splunk, ELK) for analysis. Logs are immutable — once written, they cannot be modified or deleted (only archived).

6. Knowledge Base & Data

8 questions

Desk integrates with Vault — the ODW.ai suite's knowledge base and document intelligence platform. When a customer asks a question:

  1. Desk sends the question to Vault's retrieval API.
  2. Vault searches its document corpus and returns the top 5-10 most relevant chunks (paragraphs).
  3. Desk includes these chunks in the AI's prompt, so the AI generates an answer grounded in your documents.

If you already use Vault, Desk works in minutes — your knowledge base is already there. If you don't use Vault yet, you'll need to set it up first (separate product).

Vault supports 15+ file formats:

  • Documents: PDF, Word (DOCX), PowerPoint (PPTX), Excel (XLSX), plain text (TXT, MD)
  • Web content: HTML, Markdown
  • Archives: ZIP (extracts and processes contents)
  • Media: Images (OCR), audio/video (transcription)
  • Code: Source code files (for technical documentation)
  • Other: CSV, JSON, XML

Vault uses industry-standard PRONOM format signatures to identify file types (not just file extensions), so it handles misnamed files correctly.

Vault supports incremental updates:

  • Add new documents — only new files are processed.
  • Update existing documents — only changed files are re-processed (detected via SHA-256 hash).
  • Delete documents — removed from the knowledge base immediately.

You don't need to re-process the entire knowledge base every time you make a change. Updates are fast and efficient.

You can also schedule automatic sync from folders (e.g., SharePoint, Google Drive, local filesystem) so the knowledge base stays up-to-date automatically.

Yes. Vault provides multiple ways to exclude documents:

  • Folder-level exclusion: Exclude entire folders (e.g., "HR/Confidential").
  • File-level exclusion: Exclude specific files (e.g., "salary_data.xlsx").
  • Pattern-based exclusion: Exclude files matching a pattern (e.g., "*.private").
  • Pre-processing review: Before processing, Vault generates a report of all discovered files. You can review and exclude sensitive files before they're indexed.

Excluded files are never processed or indexed. They remain in your filesystem but are invisible to the AI.

Vault uses SHA-256 hash-based deduplication:

  • Every file is fingerprinted with a SHA-256 hash (a unique digital signature).
  • If two files have the same hash, they're identical — Vault processes only one copy.
  • This works even if the files have different names or are in different folders.

Deduplication saves processing time and storage space. It also ensures the AI doesn't retrieve the same document multiple times under different names.

When Vault processes documents, it chops them into small pieces (chunks) — usually a paragraph or two. But when a chunk is isolated, it loses context. A chunk that says "the maximum was 8 degrees" has no idea whether it's talking about a slope, a temperature, or a rotation angle.

Contextual retrieval fixes this: before storing each chunk, Vault asks a small AI model to write a one-sentence context tag like "This chunk describes the slope tolerance for the delivery robot in Section 4 of the deployment manual." That tag rides along with the chunk into the vector database.

Anthropic reported a 20-30% reduction in retrieval failures using this technique. Vault implements it by default. It's one of the biggest reasons Desk's answers stay accurate on messy, real-world document collections.

Yes. Vault supports multiple collections. You can create separate collections for:

  • Departments (Sales, Support, HR, Legal)
  • Products (Product A, Product B, Product C)
  • Regions (North America, Europe, Asia-Pacific)
  • Languages (English, Chinese, Spanish)

Desk can be configured to query specific collections based on the conversation context. For example, if a customer asks about Product A, Desk queries the Product A collection. If they ask about shipping, Desk queries the Support collection.

This ensures the AI retrieves the most relevant documents and doesn't mix information from different contexts.

Vault can handle very large knowledge bases:

  • Storage: Limited only by your server's disk space and PostgreSQL/ChromaDB capacity.

Performance depends on your infrastructure. For very large knowledge bases (100k+ documents), you may need to:

  • Use a powerful server (16+ GB RAM, SSD storage)

  • Use Redis caching to speed up retrieval

For most businesses (1k-10k documents), a standard server is sufficient.

The Policy Engine runs two checks:

  1. Pre-generation hook: Before the AI generates a response, the Policy Engine checks if the customer's message triggers any policies (e.g., competitor mention). If a hard rule matches, a template response is sent immediately — the AI is skipped.
  2. Post-generation hook: After the AI generates a response, the Policy Engine validates it against active policies. If it violates a rule (e.g., mentions a competitor), the response is:
    • Rewritten: The AI is asked to regenerate the response without the violation.
    • Blocked: A template response is sent instead.
    • Escalated: The conversation is escalated to a human agent.

Every policy decision is logged in the audit trail, so you can review what happened and why.
